Marie Curie-Sklodowska

The pioneering scientist who discovered radioactivity

Discoveries

The new element was named polonium (symbol Po, atomic number 84) — referencing Marie's country of origin; the next chemical element — radium (symbol Ra, atomic number 88).

Nobel Prize

1903 - jointly with Pierre Curie and Henri Becquerel for research on radioactivity (Nobel Prize in Physics). 1911 - for studies on radium and its compounds (Nobel Prize in Chemistry).

"Nothing in life is to be feared, it is only to be understood. Now is the time to understand more, so that we may fear less."

-- Marie Curie

Marie Sklodowska-Curie, one of the most outstanding figures in the history of science, was born on November 7, 1867, in Warsaw. Her life and achievements revolutionized many fields, and her contributions to science continue to inspire generations.

Education

From a young age, Marie demonstrated extraordinary abilities. After finishing school in Warsaw, she was unable to study at Polish universities due to restrictions on women. In 1891, she moved to Paris, where she began her studies at the Sorbonne. She graduated with honors, earning a doctorate in physical sciences.

Scientific Discoveries

Together with her husband, Pierre Curie, she discovered two chemical elements: polonium and radium. Their work on radioactivity was groundbreaking and contributed to the development of radiochemistry. In 1903, for their joint research on radioactivity, Marie and Pierre Curie, along with Henri Becquerel, were awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics. Marie became the first woman to receive this award.

Further Achievements

After her husband’s death in 1906, Marie continued her research. In 1911, she received a second Nobel Prize, this time in Chemistry, for her studies on radium and its compounds. She was the first person to win Nobel Prizes in two different scientific fields.

Contribution to Medicine

Marie Skłodowska-Curie also contributed to the field of medicine, especially in radiology. Her research on radioactivity was crucial for cancer treatment. During World War I, she organized mobile radiology units that helped diagnose wounded soldiers.

Final Years and Legacy

Marie passed away on July 4, 1934, due to a radiation-related illness, likely caused by prolonged exposure to radiation. After her death, she was buried alongside her husband in the Panthéon in Paris. Her achievements and life became an inspiration for many women who sought to find their place in science.

Conclusion

Marie Skłodowska-Curie was not only an exceptional scientist but also a pioneer in the fight for gender equality in science. Her life and work demonstrate how passion and determination can lead to groundbreaking discoveries that change the world. Her legacy endures to this day, inspiring future generations of scientists.

Here's a timeline of Marie Curie's life:

  • 1867 - Born in Warsaw, Poland
  • 1891 - Moves to Paris to study at the Sorbonne University
  • 1893 - Receives a degree in Physics
  • 1894 - Begins research with Pierre Curie
  • 1898 - Discovers the elements polonium and radium
  • 1903 - Receives the Nobel Prize in Physics with Pierre Curie and Henri Becquerel
  • 1911 - Receives the Nobel Prize in Chemistry for her work on radium and polonium
  • 1934 - Dies from aplastic anemia, a condition linked to her research on radioactivity

Monument

Her successes contributed to changing the perception of women in science and served as an inspiration for many women.

Pioneering

She is the only person to have received Nobel Prizes in two different scientific fields (Physics and Chemistry).
